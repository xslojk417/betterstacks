## What Is Stacks, Actually?

The pitch is pretty bold: replace Google Drive, Notion, Pocket, Raindrop, Instapaper, your browser bookmarks, Apple Notes, and screenshots — with one workspace.

That sounds like marketing fluff until you actually look at what it does. You install the Chrome extension (Firefox works too), and from that point on, saving anything from the web takes one click. No switching tabs, no copy-pasting URLs, no choosing a folder. Stacks captures it with context — meaning it understands *why* you saved it, not just *what* you saved.

Then, instead of you having to manually organize 3,000 saved items into folders, the AI does it automatically. It builds collections, surfaces related content you didn't realize was connected, and lets you search in plain English ("that article about remote work productivity from like two months ago") and actually finds it.

It works on desktop and mobile (iOS and Android), so your saved content is always accessible wherever you are.

## What Makes It Different from Notion or Raindrop?

Notion is a great tool. So is Raindrop. But they require you to do the organizing. You create the structure, you maintain the folders, you tag things manually, you search by exact keywords.

Stacks flips that: the AI does the organizing. The search understands intent. And because it's tied to a browser extension, the *capture* part requires almost no friction — you don't have to think about where to put something, you just save it and the system figures it out.

The fact that it also handles file storage (50GB on the base plan, 500GB on higher tiers) means you can genuinely consolidate tools rather than just adding another one to the pile.
